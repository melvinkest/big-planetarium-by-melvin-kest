A Journey Through the Milky Way

The Milky Way galaxy is a vast cosmic ocean containing at least 100 billion stars, and nearly every one of those stars is a sun to its own family of planets. While our own Solar System offers a diverse cast of characters, from scorched rocky deserts to majestic ringed giants, it is only the beginning of the story.

Meet the Galactic Lineup

While every corner of our galaxy holds secrets, some worlds stand out for their extreme environments, massive sizes, or potential to host life. From our closest neighbors to exotic alien worlds thousands of light-years away, here are some of the most iconic planets currently known to science:

Earth: The Living Laboratory

The Milky Way has only one known planet, the Earth, which is capable of sustaining life mainly because it is in the adequate location in the so-called habitable zone where water can exist as a liquid. It has got a strong magnetic field earned through its spinning iron nucleus that defends the planet against the adverse radiations of the sun and stops the loss of our atmosphere.

It is also known that Earth is the only planet that has plate tectonics active; all-time motion of crustal plates of Earth recycles carbon and controls the climate on the long-term. It is a very specific, life giving combination of nitrogen and oxygen, and honed over billions of years by the very biological processes which exist in it.

Jupiter: The Galactic Vacuum Cleaner

Jupiter is a huge hydrogen and helium ball, which is big to an extent that 1,300 Earths would be contained under it. It is a defender of the inner solar system since the massive gravitational pull tends to bend out or trap loose comets and asteroids, which would otherwise fly to Earth.

The most popular aspects of the planet include the colourful strips of ammonia clouds and the most massive storm of red hue, the Great Red Spot that measures higher in pressure than our whole world and has been rotating centuries long. So much pressure is applied at the core of Jupiter that it compresses the hydrogen gas into the liquid metallic gas, and it is then a liquid metal that forms a magnetic field that is 20,000 times stronger than that on Earth.

Saturn: The Lord of the Rings

While all gas giants have rings, Saturn’s are by far the most spectacular, spanning 282,000 kilometers in width but averaging only about 10 meters in thickness. These rings are composed of trillions of particles of water ice, ranging from dust-sized grains to house-sized boulders.

Saturn itself is the least dense planet in the solar system; if you had a bathtub large enough to hold it, the planet would actually float in water. It is also a "moon king," hosting over 140 satellites, including Titan, a massive moon with a thick atmosphere and liquid methane lakes.

Proxima Centauri b: Our Closest Galactic Neighbor

Located just 4.2 light-years away, Proxima Centauri b is the nearest known planet outside our solar system. It orbits within the "habitable zone" of its star, meaning it is at the perfect distance for liquid water to potentially exist on its surface.

However, because its star is a small, cool red dwarf, the planet orbits much closer than Earth does to the Sun, completing a full year in just 11 days. Scientists believe the planet is "tidally locked," meaning one side faces its sun in eternal, scorching daylight while the other remains in a permanent, frozen night.

55 Cancri e: The Diamond Super-Earth

This is a Super-Earth, about two times larger than the planet we live on, yet a world of nothing but fire. It travels around its star so near that the temperature of its surface rises to a formidable 2,400 Degrees Celcius, which is basically a planet that is made up of molten lava.

The secret of its real popularity however, lies in its chemical composition. Researchers assume that this planet contains a lot of carbon. Because of the high temperatures and high pressure, scientists have suggested that much of its interior might consist of layers of dense diamonds, subjected to pressure, which would cause it to literally be a gem in the dark of space.

HD 189733b: The Planet Where It Rains Glass

HD 189733b is a "Hot Jupiter" located approximately 64 light-years away in the constellation Vulpecula, famously known for its striking deep blue hue and lethally exotic weather. Despite its serene appearance, which resembles Earth from a distance, the planet is a literal furnace with surface temperatures soaring above 900°C.

Its distinct cobalt color is not from water, but from a high-altitude atmosphere filled with silicate particles. Combined with screaming winds that reach speeds of up to 8,700 km/h, seven times the speed of sound; these silicates condense in the intense heat to create a terrifying meteorological phenomenon: it rains molten glass that blows sideways in the supersonic gale.
